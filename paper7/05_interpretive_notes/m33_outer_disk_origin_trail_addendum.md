# M33 Outer-Disk Origin-Trail Addendum

**Lane:** M33-Corbelli source-native thick-disk reconstruction  
**Status:** Interpretation/provenance addendum only  
**Model status:** Locked Hermes and MOND results unchanged  
**Action:** No reruns, no board edits, no age edits, no new model comparison

## Executive finding

The literature gives **moderate support** that M33's outer disk / outer H I structure is not a simple, single-lineage extension of the inner native disk. The strongest statement supported by the evidence is:

> M33's outer disk is an externally disturbed, accretion-sensitive, multi-component regime whose gas and stars do not cleanly share the same history as the inner disk.

This is stronger than a generic "warp" label, but it is not proof that all outer material is non-native or recently accreted. The evidence points to a mixed origin trail: inside-out native disk growth dominates the inner disk; the region near and beyond the break carries signatures of radial migration, possible accreted stellar material, gas accretion or reaccretion, tidal disturbance, and outer H I structures that are not well described as a settled circular disk.

This addendum does **not** change the locked interpretation:

- Hermes on the full board: pass/elevated, outer-tail underprediction.
- Hermes on the main disk: clean to near-clean.
- Hermes outer disk: severe stress.
- Fixed simple MOND: clean on the same frozen board.
- M/L scaling helps Hermes but does not rescue it.
- The M33 result remains a real Hermes stress case, not a solved Hermes success.

The value of the origin-trail audit is narrower: it clarifies that the Hermes stress zone overlaps a region where the assumption **"one galaxy = one gravitational biography = one board-level $t_{50}$"** is astrophysically fragile.

## Evidence against a simple native inside-out interpretation

The inner disk evidence does support native inside-out growth. Williams et al. find the mass fraction formed before $z=1$ drops strongly from the innermost field to the outermost inner-disk field, and they interpret the inner disk as showing inside-out growth. But the same paper marks the break around $\sim 8$ kpc and notes that beyond the truncation radius the density profile steepens and the age gradient reverses.

The outer fields complicate the simple story. Barker et al. find that the S1 field near the edge is young on average, while S2 farther out is older, more metal-poor, and much lower-mass; they explicitly state that this supports a reversal of M33's age gradient near the break radius. They also list non-native alternatives that cannot be ruled out, including halo, thick disk, or accreted substructure.

The strongest modern multi-origin clue is Mostoghiu et al. 2018: in a simulated M33 analogue, the outer age reversal is mainly due to old satellite-accreted stars, with some contribution from radial migration. The authors also state that these old accreted stars are kinematically hot and not rotationally supported, and that future outer-star kinematics would test this scenario.

So the clean conclusion is:

> M33's inner disk is consistent with native inside-out growth, but the outer disk near and beyond the break is not cleanly explained by monotonic inside-out growth alone.

## Accretion / interaction timeline

| Timescale | Evidence | Interpretation |
|---:|---|---|
| $\sim 10$ to 5 Gyr | Williams et al. report most inner stellar disk growth occurred from 10 to 5 Gyr ago. | Native inner-disk formation epoch; aligns with global $t_{50}$ scale. |
| $\sim 6.5$ Gyr | Tepper-García et al. 2020 model a close M31-M33 passage around 6.5 Gyr ago; they link it to fossil H I filament / gas-stream possibilities. | Ancient interaction scenario; contested as a complete explanation. |
| $\sim 4$–8 Gyr | Bekki 2008 models M31-M33 tidal interaction capable of producing an H I bridge and possibly fossil M33 warp signatures. | Older tidal-origin hypothesis; useful but not settled. |
| $\sim 6$ Gyr start, lasting $\sim 3$ Gyr | Javadi et al. 2017, cited by Tepper-García et al., report an enhanced M33 star-formation episode beginning around 6 Gyr ago and lasting about 3 Gyr. | Star-formation-history context, not direct outer-origin proof. |
| $z \sim 0.2$–0.4 | Barker et al. report the last major epoch of star formation at the edge of M33's disk occurred at this redshift. | Recent outer-edge SF episode; supports younger activity near the break/edge. |
| $\sim 1$–3 Gyr | Putman et al. argue M31 tidal disruption of M33's gas likely occurred in this interval, with gas now returning toward the disk. | Young disturbance/reaccretion timescale for outer gas. |
| Recent / ongoing | Corbelli & Burkert 2024 suggest more recent gas accretion, possibly from a cosmic filament, may cause outer-disk misalignment. | Most direct support for recent gas-accretion biography, but not yet a quantified accretion clock. |

No single timeline is dominant across the literature. The strongest conservative statement is that **outer gas and outer stars carry multiple plausible histories**, ranging from ancient interaction remnants and radial migration to recent gas accretion or return-flow. That is exactly the kind of situation where a single global board-level $t_{50}$ becomes physically blunt.

## Conclusion category

**Overall category:** **Moderate support** for a multi-origin gravitational biography problem.

Why not "strong support"? Because the literature does not yet prove that the specific Corbelli board points beyond $R>10$ kpc are dominated by externally accreted material with a separately measurable age/decay state. The evidence is strong that the outer disk is warped, perturbed, and population-distinct; it is moderate that the material is literally external or multi-origin in the way a component-age Hermes implementation would need.

A more precise classification is:

| Claim | Support level |
|---|---|
| The M33 outer residual zone overlaps a physically distinct outer regime. | **Strong** |
| The outer H I violates simple settled-disk assumptions through warp, twist, anomalous components, and disturbed morphology. | **Strong** |
| The outer stellar disk has a different population history than the inner disk. | **Strong** |
| The outer material is at least partly accreted / externally assembled / non-native. | **Moderate** |
| The exact local gravitational biography is younger than the global $t_{50}\approx6$ Gyr. | **Moderate for gas; weak-to-mixed for stars** |
| A component-age Hermes implementation would automatically solve M33. | **No support yet** |

## Hermes-specific payload

The origin-trail audit supports a plausible Hermes failure direction:

1. Current Hermes applies one global $t_{50}$ to the whole board.
2. The M33 main disk behaves like a coherent system under that assumption.
3. The outer disk does not: its residual zone overlaps warp, break, disturbed H I, off-rotation gas, and possible accretion/migration/accreted-star signatures.
4. If a future Hermes implementation allowed component-resolved or radial-zone-resolved histories, recently accreted or reaccreted gas could carry a younger local gravitational biography than the global M33 disk.
5. A younger local age would imply a higher local $\beta$, increasing predicted outer support.
6. The locked residual direction is underprediction, which is the direction expected if a single global age is too old / too eroded for a younger outer gas component.

But there are two critical guardrails:

- **Age alone is not guaranteed to fix M33.** The age-sweep diagnostic already showed that even the physical $\beta$ ceiling does not make Hermes competitive with MOND on the full outer disk. A future treatment would likely need both component-age and dynamical-status handling, not merely a younger number for the outer radius.
- **The stellar outer component may not be younger.** Barker S2 and Mostoghiu's simulation point toward old, metal-poor, possibly accreted or migrated stars in the far outer disk. So the local history is not simply "young outer disk." It is mixed: young/recent gas processes plus old/migrated/accreted stellar populations.

Therefore, the correct Hermes payload is:

> M33 does not merely expose a generic age-anchor uncertainty. It exposes a possible mismatch between a single global age parameter and a multi-origin outer disk whose gas and stars may not share the inner disk's formation and dynamical history.

## Paper-safe framing

> The M33 origin-trail audit strengthens the interpretation that the locked Hermes residual is spatially and astrophysically localized. Hermes fits the main disk cleanly, but the stressed $R>10$ kpc region overlaps M33's warp onset, disk break, far-outer CMD population reversal, disturbed H I, off-rotation gas, and published accretion or interaction scenarios. This does not erase the fixed-MOND comparator result, which remains clean on the same frozen board, and it does not prove that Hermes is correct to reject the outer disk. Rather, it identifies M33 as a boundary case for the current one-galaxy/one-age implementation. A future dynamic-aging Hermes extension would need to treat component origin, radial age, and circular-equilibrium status explicitly; that extension is outside the executable scope of the present paper.
